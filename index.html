Revise By Period

c.1000-c.1500

1. Medieval Britain

Crime, punishment, and law enforcement were local, religious, and community-based.

Key Crimes

  • Theft was common because most people were poor.
  • Poaching and forest offences became more serious after Norman control.
  • Treason challenged the monarch and was treated as especially dangerous.
  • Heresy threatened Church authority and could be punished severely.

Law Enforcement

  • Tithings made groups of men responsible for each other.
  • The hue and cry expected villagers to chase suspects.
  • Trial by ordeal relied on God to show innocence or guilt until 1215.
  • Juries and royal courts developed after ordeal ended.

Punishment

  • Fines, stocks, pillory, whipping, and execution were public deterrents.
  • The Church could offer sanctuary and benefit of clergy.
  • Norman punishments were harsher, including mutilation and forest law penalties.
Exam argument: Continuity mattered because communities still did most law enforcement, but Norman government increased royal control and harsher punishments.
c.1500-c.1700

2. Early Modern Britain

Religious change, population growth, and poverty created new fears about social order.

Key Crimes

  • Vagrancy rose as unemployment, inflation, and enclosure increased poverty.
  • Smuggling and poaching were seen by many ordinary people as survival crimes.
  • Witchcraft prosecutions grew during periods of fear and religious tension.
  • Treason was linked to religious conflict under Tudor and Stuart monarchs.

Law Enforcement

  • Constables and watchmen remained unpaid local officials.
  • Justices of the Peace gained importance in local government.
  • Printed pamphlets spread fear about criminals and witches.

Punishment

  • The Bloody Code began expanding the number of capital crimes.
  • Vagrants could be whipped, branded, or sent to houses of correction.
  • Public punishment aimed to shame offenders and deter crowds.
Exam argument: Attitudes were central: fear of disorder made minor crimes seem threatening, so punishment often became harsher even when law enforcement stayed local.
c.1700-c.1900

3. Industrial Britain

Industrialisation, urbanisation, and new ideas changed policing and punishment dramatically.

Key Crimes

  • Highway robbery became feared because roads, trade, and travel increased.
  • Smuggling grew due to high taxes on goods such as tea and brandy.
  • Poaching became a conflict between landowners and rural workers.
  • Urban theft increased as towns grew rapidly.

Law Enforcement

  • Bow Street Runners improved thief-taking in London from 1749.
  • Robert Peel's Metropolitan Police was created in 1829.
  • County and borough police forces expanded later in the nineteenth century.
  • Detectives, records, and photography made policing more professional.

Punishment

  • Transportation sent convicts first to America, then Australia.
  • Prisons became more important after transportation declined.
  • Separate and silent systems aimed to reform prisoners but were harsh.
  • The Bloody Code was reduced as attitudes moved towards reform.
Exam argument: Government became more active because old local systems could not cope with larger cities and changing crime patterns.
c.1900-present

4. Modern Britain

Technology, welfare, changing attitudes, and specialist policing transformed crime and punishment.

Key Crimes

  • Car crime, cybercrime, hate crime, and terrorism reflect modern society.
  • Conscientious objection challenged wartime expectations.
  • Domestic violence and race hate became treated more seriously as attitudes changed.
  • Drug offences became a major policing issue in the twentieth century.

Law Enforcement

  • Police use radios, cars, CCTV, databases, DNA, and forensic science.
  • Specialist units deal with terrorism, cybercrime, drugs, and serious violence.
  • Neighbourhood policing tries to maintain public trust.

Punishment

  • The death penalty was abolished for murder in 1965 and fully later.
  • Prison remains important, but rehabilitation and community sentences expanded.
  • Young offenders are treated differently from adults.
  • Probation and parole reflect a greater focus on reform.
Exam argument: Modern change is often driven by technology and changing attitudes, but debate continues between punishment, deterrence, and rehabilitation.
c.1870-c.1900

5. Whitechapel Historic Environment

Whitechapel tests how well you can use sources and knowledge of a specific place.

Local Conditions

  • Overcrowded lodging houses made life unstable and difficult to police.
  • Poverty, unemployment, alcohol, and prostitution shaped crime and vulnerability.
  • Immigration created tension and prejudice, especially towards Jewish migrants.
  • Narrow courts and alleys made surveillance difficult.

Policing Problems

  • The Metropolitan Police and City Police had overlapping responsibilities.
  • Witnesses were often unreliable or afraid.
  • Forensic science was limited compared with today.
  • Press coverage could create panic and spread rumours.

Sources

  • Use provenance: who made it, when, why, and for which audience?
  • Link source details to Whitechapel knowledge.
  • Consider what the source leaves out.
Exam argument: Whitechapel's environment made crime harder to prevent and investigate, so sources must be judged against local poverty, prejudice, policing limits, and press influence.
Across the course

6. Big Factors

The strongest answers compare factors across periods instead of describing one event at a time.

Government: more laws, police forces, prisons, and national systems over time.
Religion: huge medieval and early modern influence; weaker as society secularised.
Poverty: explains theft, vagrancy, poaching, lodging-house life, and some urban crime.
Attitudes: changing views on witchcraft, the death penalty, youth crime, domestic abuse, and rehabilitation.
Technology: roads helped highwaymen, but later transport, communications, CCTV, DNA, and databases helped police.
Individuals: Robert Peel, Elizabeth Fry, John Howard, Henry and John Fielding, and reform campaigners.
Exam argument: No single factor explains everything. The best answers show which factor mattered most in the period named by the question.

Exam Technique

How To Answer Edexcel Questions

Always check the command word, time period, and marks. The examiner rewards precise knowledge used to make a judgement.

4 marks: describe two features

Write two developed features. Each feature needs a clear point and specific detail.

Feature + precise detail = 2 marks

Example: One feature of medieval law enforcement was the hue and cry. Villagers were expected to raise the alarm and chase a suspect together.

12 marks: explain why

Use three explained reasons. Link each reason directly to the question and include period detail.

Reason + evidence + explanation + link

Good paragraphs use words like "because", "this meant", and "therefore" to show causation.

16 marks: how far do you agree?

Make a balanced judgement. Discuss the statement and at least one other factor, then decide which mattered most.

Agree + challenge + compare + judgement

Your final judgement should explain why one factor was more important, not simply repeat the question.

Whitechapel source questions

Use source content, provenance, and your own knowledge together. Do not just copy the source.

Source detail + own knowledge + usefulness limit

For "follow-up enquiry", choose a question that is focused and name a source that would help answer it.

Practice

Questions To Train With

Use these for timed practice, essay plans, flashcards, or paragraph drills.

4 marks

Describe two features of the medieval hue and cry.

Plan two separate features: community responsibility and the expectation to chase suspects.

4 marks

Describe two features of transportation as a punishment.

Think about why it was used and what happened after America was no longer available.

12 marks

Explain why vagabondage was treated as a serious crime in the sixteenth century.

Use poverty, population growth, fear of disorder, and government attitudes.

12 marks

Explain why policing changed in the nineteenth century.

Use urbanisation, limits of local law enforcement, Peel, and changing government responsibility.

12 marks

Explain why the death penalty was used less often from c.1700 to the present.

Use changing attitudes, prison reform, miscarriages of justice, and alternatives.

16 marks

"The main reason punishment changed was the work of individuals." How far do you agree?

Compare individuals with government, attitudes, and technology across time.

16 marks

"Religion was the most important factor affecting crime and punishment in the period c.1000-c.1700." How far do you agree?

Balance religion against monarchy, local communities, poverty, and fear of disorder.

Whitechapel

How useful are Sources A and B for an enquiry into policing Whitechapel?

Use content, provenance, local conditions, and limits of police knowledge.

Whitechapel

Suggest one follow-up enquiry into living conditions in Whitechapel.

Make the enquiry focused, then name a source such as lodging-house records, census data, police reports, or newspaper accounts.

Top Tips

How To Revise Smarter

Use period contrasts

Learn what stays the same and what changes between periods. This is the core of a thematic study.

Anchor every paragraph

Use exact examples: hue and cry, benefit of clergy, witchcraft, Bow Street Runners, Peel's police, transportation, separate system, DNA.

Rank your factors

For judgement questions, decide which factor had the biggest effect in that period and why the others mattered less.

Practise source thinking

For Whitechapel, ask: what does this source show, why was it produced, and what local knowledge helps me test it?

Make mini timelines

Put policing and punishment changes in order. Sequence helps you explain change rather than just list facts.

Write fewer vague sentences

Replace "things changed a lot" with a clear mechanism: "urban growth made unpaid local policing inadequate."
